What is Phishing?

Phishing is a cyberattack where criminals send fake emails, messages, or create fake websites to trick people into giving away passwords, credit card numbers, or personal information.

⚠️ 91% of all cyberattacks start with a phishing email. Over 3.4 billion phishing emails are sent every single day.

📧 Email Phishing

What is it? Email phishing is the most common type of phishing attack. Attackers send fake emails that look like they are from trusted companies — banks, PayPal, Amazon, Google, or even your employer.

How do they do it? They create email addresses that look almost real (e.g. support@paypa1.com instead of paypal.com). The email usually contains a link to a fake login page where your credentials are stolen the moment you enter them. They use fear, urgency, and official-looking logos to make the email look genuine.

What do they want? Your username, password, credit card number, OTP, or personal details.
📩 Real-World Example — Fake PayPal Email
From: security@paypa1-support.com
To: you@gmail.com
Subject: URGENT: Your PayPal account is suspended!

Dear Valued Customer,

We detected suspicious activity on your account. Verify your details within 24 hours or your account will be permanently closed.

🔗 Click here to verify your account now

Regards,
PayPal Security Team

🚩 "paypa1" uses number 1 instead of letter l — fake domain
🚩 "URGENT" + "24 hours" — creates panic so you don't think clearly
🚩 "Valued Customer" — real PayPal always uses your actual name
🚩 Threat of permanent closure — forces you to click without thinking

📱 Smishing (SMS Phishing)

What is it? Smishing is phishing done through SMS text messages. The word comes from "SMS + Phishing." Attackers send text messages pretending to be from banks, delivery companies, or government agencies.

How do they do it? They send a short, alarming text with a link. The link goes to a fake website that steals your login credentials or installs malware on your phone. They may also ask you to call a fake number where a scammer awaits.

What do they want? Bank login details, OTPs, or to install malware on your phone to steal data.
📩 Real-World Example — Fake SBI Bank SMS
SBI-ALERT: Your account has been temporarily blocked due to suspicious activity. Verify now to avoid permanent suspension:

http://sbi-verify-now.com/login

— SBI Customer Care
🚩 Real banks NEVER send login links via SMS
🚩 "sbi-verify-now.com" is NOT the official SBI website (onlinesbi.com)
🚩 "Blocked account" creates fear to make you click immediately
✅ Call your bank directly using the number on the back of your card — never use the link in an SMS

📞 Vishing (Voice Phishing)

What is it? Vishing is phishing over a phone call. "Vishing" comes from "Voice + Phishing." Attackers call pretending to be from tech support, your bank, the government (income tax department), or even the police.

How do they do it? They use caller ID spoofing to make the call look like it is from a real company. They create urgency and fear — claiming your computer has a virus, your account is hacked, or you owe taxes. They then ask for remote access to your computer, your OTP, or a payment to "fix" the issue.

What do they want? Remote access to your computer, OTPs, bank details, or direct money transfers.
📩 Real-World Example — Fake Microsoft Support Call
Caller: "Hello, this is John from Microsoft Technical Support. We have detected a dangerous virus on your Windows computer. Your files and bank details are at risk right now."

Caller: "I need you to download a small tool so we can connect to your computer and remove the virus. This is completely free — but you must act NOW before the virus spreads further."

Caller: "Please give me your Windows login password so I can begin the fix immediately."
🚩 Microsoft NEVER calls you without you contacting them first
🚩 Asking for remote access or your password is always a scam
🚩 "Act NOW" — urgency tactic to stop you from thinking or verifying
✅ Hang up immediately. No company will ever call you unsolicited about a virus on your PC.

🎯 Spear Phishing

What is it? Spear phishing is a targeted phishing attack aimed at a specific person. Unlike regular phishing which is sent to thousands of random people, spear phishing is crafted for YOU specifically.

How do they do it? The attacker first researches you on LinkedIn, Facebook, or your company website. They find your name, job title, employer, city, and colleagues. Then they send a highly personalized email that mentions specific details about your life — making it feel completely genuine and trustworthy.

What do they want? Your login credentials, Aadhaar/PAN details, bank information, or access to company systems.
📩 Real-World Example — Fake Job Offer (researched from LinkedIn)
From: rahul.hr@infosys-careers.net
To: priya.patel@gmail.com
Subject: Job Offer — Senior Developer at Infosys, Pune

Hi Priya,

I came across your LinkedIn profile and was impressed by your 4 years of Python experience at TCS, Pune. We have a Senior Developer opening at Infosys with ₹18 LPA package.

Please fill the attached form with your Aadhaar number and bank account details for our background verification process to proceed.

Regards,
Rahul Sharma — HR, Infosys

🚩 Uses your real name, employer, city and skills — stolen from LinkedIn
🚩 "infosys-careers.net" is fake — real domain is infosys.com
🚩 No real company asks for Aadhaar + bank details before an interview
✅ Verify job offers directly on the company's official website or call their HR department

🐋 Whaling (CEO Fraud)

What is it? Whaling targets high-value individuals — CEOs, CFOs, directors, or senior managers. The name comes from "going after the big fish." These attacks often result in huge financial losses — sometimes crores of rupees.

How do they do it? The attacker impersonates the CEO or a senior executive and emails a finance or HR employee. They create a sense of secrecy and urgency — asking for urgent wire transfers, employee salary details, or W-2 tax forms. Since the email appears to come from the boss, employees often comply without questioning.

What do they want? Large wire transfers, sensitive employee data, or access to company financial systems.
📩 Real-World Example — Fake CEO Wire Transfer Request
From: amit.verma.ceo@techcorp-global.net
To: sanjay.finance@techcorp.com
Subject: Urgent — Confidential Wire Transfer

Hi Sanjay,

I am in a confidential board meeting and cannot take calls right now. I need you to urgently process a wire transfer of ₹45,00,000 to our new vendor account for a deal closing today.

Account: HDFC Bank — 12345678901 | IFSC: HDFC0001234

Do not discuss this with anyone — it is strictly confidential. I will explain everything when I return.

— Amit Verma, CEO

🚩 "techcorp-global.net" is a fake domain — not the real company email
🚩 "Do not discuss with anyone" — isolates the victim to bypass approval process
🚩 Urgency + confidentiality stops the employee from verifying the request
✅ Always verify large transfers via a direct phone call to the sender — never trust email alone

🌐 Fake Website (Pharming)

What is it? Fake websites (also called pharming) are websites designed to look exactly like real ones — same logo, same layout, same colors. The only difference is the URL. Victims are sent to these sites via phishing emails, SMS links, or even search engine ads.

How do they do it? Attackers buy a domain that looks similar to the real one (e.g. hdfcbank-login.com instead of hdfcbank.com) and copy the entire design of the real website. When you enter your username and password, it goes directly to the attacker. Some advanced attacks even intercept DNS requests to redirect you to a fake site even when you type the correct URL.

What do they want? Your bank login, credit card details, or any account credentials.
📩 Real-World Example — Fake HDFC Bank Login Page
🔴
http://hdfc-bank-login.com/secure/netbanking

HDFC Bank — NetBanking Login

Secure Customer Portal

🚩 URL is "hdfc-bank-login.com" — the real site is "hdfcbank.com"
🚩 Uses HTTP not HTTPS — no padlock icon means not secure
🚩 Looks identical to the real website — very convincing to victims
✅ Always check the full URL before entering any login details
✅ Bookmark your bank's official website and always use that bookmark

How to Stay Safe

✅ Always check the sender's full email address carefully — not just the display name
✅ Never click links in emails or SMS — type the website address directly in your browser
✅ Enable Two-Factor Authentication (2FA) on all important accounts
✅ Never share your password, OTP, or Aadhaar number via email, SMS, or phone
✅ When in doubt, call the company directly using their official number from their website
✅ Report suspicious emails to your IT department immediately
✅ Keep your browser, antivirus, and operating system updated at all times
